A leadership philosophy by Robert K. Greenleaf first introduced in the essay "The Servant as Leader" published in 1970 that I like to apply and find is often misunderstood... here's what it means to me and why I try to consciously learn and apply it in work and life...

Firstly, what it isn't...

Servant leadership isn't about being an easy or soft leader unable to make decisions. Or literally "serving" people doing their work. Nor does it mean you won't have to make difficult decisions or any decisions at all. It's far more complex than simply being "nice" (not that I'm adverse to being nice! Everyone should be whether at work or not).

So what is it?

For me it's about "excellence, the ruthless persuit of perfection, the urgency to achieve the mission, constant dissatisfaction with the status quo and a zeal for helping those around you" to mostly quote Gene Kim. I had to go through a number of realisations to truly appreciate and understand it:

  • A leader's availability/capacity (or lack of) should not block, slow or stall progress
  • The further decisions are made from where they need to be, the poorer they will be
  • The further decisions are made from where they need to be, the slower they will be
  • A single point of failure (not just technical, but human too) can cause an entire system to stop
  • "Two heads are better than one"

The aim... to get the best out of those around you by putting them before yourself, building trust... and inspiring, empowering, supporting, and guiding in order to build a team of leaders. It's by no means an easy philosophy to learn/apply as it heavily relies on empathy, observation, awareness, influence and foresight. But beyond being a more enjoyable and rewarding way of working (for me), there are numerous benefits and performance improvements, some proven in neuroscientific studies:

"Compared with people at low-trust companies, people at high-trust companies report: 74% less stress, 106% more energy at work, 50% higher productivity, 13% fewer sick days, 76% more engagement, 29% more satisfaction with their lives, 40% less burnout."

In examples...

  • A team comes to me to make a decision around frontend architecture options. Am I best placed to? Nope. Do I have the necessary knowledge or involvment relative to those doing the work? Nope. Could I guide and facilitate the team to make a decision. Yes!
  • The team need to make a decision fast but I'm on holiday/in meetings/at lunch/sick etc... Do they heavily rely on me so wait until I'm back to make a decision? If yes, then I'm blocking progress. Or do they feel confident, empowered and prepared enough to make a decision? If not, then I'm failing as a leader, am a dependency, single point of failure and delaying progress.
  • We discuss a problem. I put forward my decision (a terrible choice). Am I human? Yes. Will I make mistakes? Yes. However, the other team members grown, empowered and experienced in such situations point out the failings in my choice. We collectively make a better decision with a better outcome.

My aim...as a servant leader, if I were to suddenly magicly disappear and the team (after the shock, if any, of me disappearing) could not only maintain but continue to improve their performance and way of working, then I've been a successful leader. Simple.
